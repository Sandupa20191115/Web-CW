What Is Physics ?

This is the study of natural science. The word physics comes from the ancient Greek word physikḗ meaning : the knowledge and study of all natural things.

The Main Goal?

Its main goal is to understand how the universe and its components work. The work in physics can be vary from the simplest thing to the most complicated. As from spinning electrons to spinning galaxies. From electrical impulses to Black Holes.

Core Theories In Physics

Since physics includes a vas variety of sciences and systems, a certain set o theories are being used by physicist : a scientist who specializes in physics. These concepts and theories has been implemented , tested and proven accurate to an adequate amount of the physical world and some theories even hypothetical worlds. These main theories are important tools for newer research and newer concepts.

   1.Classical Physics

This includes the traditional of traditional concepts that were recognized and developed before the 20th century. Most of the classical physics is involved with bodies acted by force and bodies in motion.

   2.Modern Physics

Classical physics is generally concerned with matter and energy. This field of study came up with Max Plank’s and Albert Einstein’s work with particles and relativity.

The two chief theories of modern physics present a different picture of the concepts of space, time, and matter from that presented by classical physics. Classical mechanics approximates nature as continuous, while quantum theory is concerned with the discrete nature of many phenomena at the atomic and subatomic level and with the complementary aspects of particles and waves in the description of such phenomena. Thermodynamics

This is the branch is classical physics that deal with heat and temperature. The behavior of these quantities are bound by the laws of thermodynamics.
-Zeroth Law
If two systems are both in thermal equilibrium with a third system then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other.
-First Law
The law of conservation of energy states that the total energy of an isolated system is constant; energy can be transformed from one form to another, but can be neither created nor destroyed.
-Second Law
When two initially isolated systems in separate but nearby regions of space, each in thermodynamic equilibrium with itself but not necessarily with each other, are then allowed to interact, they will eventually reach a mutual thermodynamic equilibrium. The sum of the entropies of the initially isolated systems is less than or equal to the total entropy of the final combination. Equality occurs just when the two original systems have all their respective intensive variables (temperature, pressure) equal; then the final system also has the same values.
-Third Law
The entropy of a perfect crystal of any pure substance approaches zero as the temperature approaches absolute zero

Electromagnetism

Electromagnetism is a branch of physics involving the study of the electromagnetic force, a type of physical interaction that occurs between electrically charged particles. This electromagnetic field is carried out by an electro magnetic field , A combined field consisting both electro and magnetic fields separating and co existing. Electromagnetism is learned and taught with many many methods. Let’s look at some briefly.
-Lorentz Force
In physics (specifically in electromagnetism) the Lorentz force (or electromagnetic force) is the combination of electric and magnetic force on a point charge due to electromagnetic fields
-Lenz’s Law
Lenz's law, named after the physicist Emil Lenz states that the direction of the electric current which is induced in a conductor by a changing magnetic field is such that the magnetic field created by the induced current opposes the initial changing magnetic field.
-Max’s Law
The Maxwell stress tensor (named after James Clerk Maxwell) is a symmetric second-order tensor used in classical electromagnetism to represent the interaction between electromagnetic forces and mechanical momentum
-Faraday’s Law
Faraday's law of induction (briefly, Faraday's law) is a basic law of electromagnetism predicting how a magnetic field will interact with an electric circuit to produce an electromotive force (EMF)—a phenomenon known as electromagnetic induction. This is highly used in transformers, inductors and mortors.
